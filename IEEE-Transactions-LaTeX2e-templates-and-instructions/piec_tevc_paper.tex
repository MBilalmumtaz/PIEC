\begin{abstract}
%% ============================================================================
Real-time path planning for autonomous ground vehicles (UGVs) in dynamic
environments requires resolving competing objectives---energy consumption, path
length, curvature, obstacle clearance, localization uncertainty, and
stability---within strict millisecond replanning budgets. Standard multi-objective
evolutionary algorithms (MOEAs) cannot meet these budgets: accurate physics-based
energy evaluation costs 1.26\,ms per trajectory segment, and cold-start
populations waste evaluations on solutions unrelated to the current environment
state.
We propose \emph{RT-NSGA-II}, a real-time NSGA-II framework that addresses these
challenges through three complementary contributions:
(i)~an explicit \emph{constraint-handling strategy} based on feasibility-first
selection that prioritizes collision-free, kinematically valid solutions without
requiring sensitive penalty weights, reducing constraint violations by 41\%
compared to standard penalty formulations;
(ii)~a \emph{surrogate evaluation} mechanism using a compact neural energy
predictor that delivers a 3.7$\times$ inference speedup over full physics
simulation while maintaining 92\% relative accuracy, enabling 480 fitness
evaluations within a single 100\,ms planning cycle; and
(iii)~a \emph{warm-start} initialization strategy that seeds each planning cycle
from the Pareto-optimal solutions of the preceding cycle, reducing the required
generations by 58\% by exploiting temporal continuity in the robot's environment.
Validated on a Scout Mini differential-drive UGV equipped with a 32-beam LiDAR
and 9-axis IMU across 500 simulation trials and 200 real-world experiments,
RT-NSGA-II achieves 20.1\% energy reduction versus A* (36.1\,J/m vs.\ 45.2\,J/m),
97\% mission success, a hypervolume indicator of 0.73 on the seven-objective Pareto
front, and 94.7\,ms NSGA-II planning time per cycle---demonstrating that
constraint-aware feasibility selection and surrogate-assisted evaluation together
resolve the computational bottleneck that prevents standard MOEAs from operating
on embedded robotic hardware in real time.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% ============================================================================

\IEEEPARstart{A}{utonomous} ground vehicles (UGVs) operating in dynamic
environments must continuously replan safe, energy-efficient trajectories within
strict on-board computational budgets. From an evolutionary computation
perspective, this constitutes a \emph{time-critical, constrained multi-objective
optimization problem}: each replanning cycle must respect hard feasibility
constraints (collision avoidance, kinematic limits) while simultaneously
optimizing competing objectives including energy consumption, path length,
curvature, obstacle clearance, localization uncertainty, trajectory deviation,
and stability~\cite{Mei2005,Tokekar2014}.
A 20\% reduction in energy consumption extends operational time by approximately
25\%, making energy-aware planning of direct practical importance across warehouse
automation, planetary exploration, search-and-rescue, and last-mile delivery.

\textbf{Limitations of existing approaches.}
Classical planners---A*~\cite{Hart1968}, D*~\cite{Stentz1995},
RRT*~\cite{Karaman2011}, and CHOMP~\cite{Ratliff2009}---optimize purely geometric
metrics and are blind to energy dynamics (rolling resistance $F_r = C_r m g
\cos\alpha$, gravitational work $F_g = mg\sin\alpha$, motor efficiency curves).
Asymptotically optimal sampling-based planners~\cite{Gammell2021rrt} provide
length guarantees but offer no mechanism for multi-objective energy--safety
trade-off exploration.
Learning-based navigation~\cite{Xiao2022nav,Zhu2022drlnav,Lu2025icra} avoids
explicit planning but requires large training datasets, lacks energy optimality
guarantees, and faces sim-to-real transfer challenges.
Energy-efficient planning for Ackermann-steering vehicles~\cite{Zhang2023} uses
specialized analytical models to achieve promising energy savings, though without
multi-objective Pareto search or systematic constraint handling.

\textbf{MOEAs for path planning.}
Multi-objective evolutionary algorithms (MOEAs)---particularly
NSGA-II~\cite{Deb2002}, NSGA-III~\cite{Deb2014}, and MOEA/D~\cite{Zhang2007}---
are natural fits for multi-objective path planning: population-based Pareto search
discovers diverse energy--safety--stability trade-offs inaccessible to
weighted-sum scalarization~\cite{Zheng2023moea,Gu2022mopp,Faizullin2025mo}.
Zheng et al.~\cite{Zheng2023moea} demonstrate terrain-adaptive cost functions
in evolutionary path planning but rely on simplified geometric energy estimates
rather than simulation-validated surrogates.
Gu et al.~\cite{Gu2022mopp} address dynamic obstacle environments with
MOEA replanning but at 0.5\,Hz rates insufficient for fast-moving obstacles,
constrained by expensive simulation evaluation.
Faizullin et al.~\cite{Faizullin2025mo} apply multi-criteria evolutionary
optimization to agricultural robot routing under soil compaction constraints,
achieving significant energy savings on specialized terrain.
However, none of these works meet the sub-100\,ms replanning budgets required
for online robotic deployment.

\textbf{Constraint handling in MOEAs.}
Hard safety constraints---collision avoidance, minimum clearance, kinematic
feasibility---are not naturally handled by naive penalty formulations, especially
when feasibility is discontinuous and violations cannot be tolerated.
NSGA-II's original design assumes objectives and constraints are smooth and
separable; in practice, feasibility pressure in constrained settings requires
dedicated selection strategies that prioritize feasible solutions independently
of their objective values~\cite{Deb2002,Chen2024safety}.
Without explicit constraint handling, evolutionary planners may converge to
high-quality but infeasible solutions, particularly in cluttered environments.

\textbf{Surrogate-assisted evolutionary optimization.}
Surrogate-assisted multi-objective optimization has gained traction for expensive
engineering problems~\cite{Liu2024surrogate,Tian2023pairwise,Si2025surrogate}.
Tian et al.~\cite{Tian2023pairwise} show that pairwise comparison surrogate
models reduce expensive evaluations by 40--60\% while maintaining Pareto front
quality.
Liu et al.~\cite{Liu2024surrogate} propose adaptive surrogate-based environmental
selection, achieving order-of-magnitude speedups on benchmark functions.
Si et al.~\cite{Si2025surrogate} establish linear subspace surrogate models for
large-scale many-objective optimization.
Multi-objective Bayesian optimization~\cite{Daulton2022moo} addresses expensive
objectives via probabilistic surrogate modeling, but targets offline batch
optimization rather than real-time sequential decision-making.
Integrating surrogate evaluation into a real-time, constrained MOEA remains
non-trivial: approximation errors can mis-rank solutions and degrade feasibility,
requiring careful verification against hard constraints.

\textbf{Real-time evolutionary optimization.}
Most MOEA-based planning works compute paths offline or tolerate multi-second
latencies~\cite{Zheng2023moea,Gu2022mopp}.
Online replanning for autonomous vehicles~\cite{Chen2024safety} uses deterministic
optimization rather than evolutionary search.
Recent deep reinforcement learning~\cite{GAO20251,Lu2025icra} addresses mobile
robot energy optimization but requires extensive offline training and provides
no Pareto trade-off analysis.
The closest precedent to our work is the class of anytime evolutionary
algorithms~\cite{Deb2002} that return the best current solution when interrupted---
our approach extends this paradigm by (i)~exploiting temporal solution
structure through warm-start initialization to improve anytime quality, and
(ii)~combining explicit constraint handling with surrogate evaluation to maximize
useful Pareto search within a fixed time budget.

\textbf{This paper.}
We introduce RT-NSGA-II, a real-time multi-objective path planning framework that
makes Pareto-based evolutionary search practical on embedded robotic hardware by
combining explicit constraint handling with surrogate evaluation and warm-start
initialization.
The key insight is that \emph{temporal continuity in robot navigation}---the
Pareto-optimal path at time $t$ is typically a small perturbation of the path at
time $t-\Delta t$---creates a structural opportunity absent from generic MOEA
benchmarks: re-seeding each cycle from the previous Pareto front dramatically
reduces the generations needed for convergence.
A compact neural surrogate replaces expensive physics simulation, unlocking
deeper generational search; and a feasibility-first selection mechanism ensures
that constraint satisfaction is maintained under the tight per-cycle budget.

\textbf{Contributions.} The specific contributions of this paper are:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Constraint-handling strategy} (Section~\ref{sec:mutation}):
    A feasibility-first selection mechanism that prioritizes collision-free,
    kinematically valid solutions without penalty weights, combined with a
    clearance-aware waypoint mutation operator that reduces constraint violations
    by 41\% and improves average path clearance from 0.52\,m to 0.67\,m.

  \item \textbf{Surrogate-assisted evaluation} (Section~\ref{sec:surrogate}):
    A compact 3-hidden-layer feedforward neural network trained offline on
    50,000 simulation trajectories provides 3.7$\times$ speedup over full
    physics simulation per sequential query, with batch inference providing
    up to $10\times$ speedup---enabling 480 fitness evaluations within the
    94.7\,ms NSGA-II planning budget.

  \item \textbf{Warm-start population initialization} (Section~\ref{sec:warmstart}):
    We formalize temporal Pareto reuse and prove that warm-start seeding reduces
    the expected number of generations for $\epsilon$-convergence by a factor
    proportional to the temporal autocorrelation of the Pareto front.
    Empirically, warm-start reduces required generations from 30 to 12 (58\%
    reduction) while improving hypervolume by 7.4\%.

  \item \textbf{Seven-objective path planning formulation}
    (Section~\ref{sec:formulation}): A rigorous formulation of UGV path planning
    as a seven-objective problem (energy, length, curvature, obstacle cost,
    localization uncertainty, deviation, stability) with formal proof of
    Pareto incompatibility between energy minimization and safety maximization.

  \item \textbf{Empirical validation} (Section~\ref{sec:results}): Comprehensive
    evaluation across 500 simulation trials and 200 real-world experiments on
    a Scout Mini UGV, demonstrating state-of-the-art energy efficiency (20.1\%
    reduction vs.\ A*) and mission reliability (97\% success).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:formulation}
%% ============================================================================

\subsection{Seven-Objective Path Optimization}

Let $\mathcal{P} = \{(x_0,y_0),(x_1,y_1),\ldots,(x_n,y_n)\}$ denote a piecewise-linear
path with $n \le 8$ waypoints in a 2-D occupancy grid map $\mathcal{M}$.
RT-NSGA-II minimizes the vector objective:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{f}(\mathcal{P})
  = \bigl[f_1,f_2,f_3,f_4,f_5,f_6,f_7\bigr]^\top
  = \bigl[L,\kappa,C_{\mathrm{obs}},U,D,E,T\bigr]^\top
  \label{eq:objectives}
\end{equation}
subject to the constraints: $\mathcal{P}$ is collision-free,
$d_{\mathrm{clear}}(x_i,y_i) \ge d^*$ for all $i$ (minimum clearance
$d^* = 0.4$\,m), and start/goal endpoints are fixed.
The seven objectives are:
\begin{align}
  f_1 &= L(\mathcal{P})
       = \sum_{i=0}^{n-1}\|(x_{i+1},y_{i+1})-(x_i,y_i)\|
       \label{eq:f1} \\
  f_2 &= \kappa(\mathcal{P})
       = \frac{1}{n-2}\sum_{i=1}^{n-2}
         \arccos\!\left(\frac{\mathbf{v}_i\cdot\mathbf{v}_{i+1}}
         {\|\mathbf{v}_i\|\|\mathbf{v}_{i+1}\|}\right)
       \label{eq:f2} \\
  f_3 &= C_{\mathrm{obs}}(\mathcal{P})
       = \sum_{i=0}^{n} 6.0\cdot\max\bigl(0,\,0.4-d_{\mathrm{clear}}(x_i,y_i)\bigr)^2
       \label{eq:f3} \\
  f_4 &= U(\mathcal{P})
       = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=0}^{n}\mathrm{tr}\!\bigl(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\mathrm{pos}}(x_i,y_i)\bigr)
       \label{eq:f4} \\
  f_5 &= D(\mathcal{P})
       = \frac{|L(\mathcal{P})-L_{\mathrm{straight}}|}
              {\max(L_{\mathrm{straight}},0.1)}
       \label{eq:f5} \\
  f_6 &= E(\mathcal{P})
       = \sum_{i=0}^{n-1}\hat{E}(\boldsymbol{u}_i)
       \label{eq:f6} \\
  f_7 &= T(\mathcal{P})
       = 1 - \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=0}^{n-1}\hat{S}(\boldsymbol{u}_i)
       \label{eq:f7}
\end{align}
where $\boldsymbol{u}_i = [x_i,y_i,\theta_i,v_i,\omega_i,\boldsymbol{\xi}_i,l_{\mathrm{path},i}]^\top$
is the 11-dimensional surrogate input (Eq.~\ref{eq:surrogate_input}),
$\hat{E}(\boldsymbol{u}_i) \ge 0$ is the predicted average power and
$\hat{S}(\boldsymbol{u}_i) \in [0,1]$ is the predicted stability score,
both outputs of the compact neural surrogate (Section~\ref{sec:surrogate}),
$\boldsymbol{\xi}_i$ encodes terrain features (slope, roughness, obstacle
density, clearance), $l_{\mathrm{path},i}$ is the segment path length, and
$\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\mathrm{pos}}$ is the position covariance estimated by the
Unscented Kalman Filter localization module.

\subsection{Computational Bottleneck}

Evaluating $E(\mathcal{P})$ via full physics simulation requires numerical
integration of the differential-drive dynamics:
\begin{equation}
  \dot{\mathbf{x}} = \begin{bmatrix}v\cos\theta \\ v\sin\theta \\ \omega\end{bmatrix},
  \qquad
  P(t) = \frac{F_r(t) + F_g(t) + F_d(t)}{\eta_m}\cdot v(t)
  \label{eq:dynamics}
\end{equation}
where $F_r = C_r mg\cos\alpha$, $F_g = mg\sin\alpha$, $F_d = \tfrac{1}{2}C_d\rho A v^2$,
$\eta_m$ is motor efficiency, and $E = \int_0^T P(t)\,dt$.
This 4th-order Runge--Kutta integration costs $t_{\mathrm{sim}} = 1.26$\,ms
per path segment.
For a standard NSGA-II run with $N_{\mathrm{pop}}=100$ individuals,
$N_{\mathrm{gen}}=30$ generations, and $n=8$ waypoints per path,
total evaluation time is:
\begin{equation}
  T_{\mathrm{sim}} = N_{\mathrm{pop}} \times N_{\mathrm{gen}} \times n \times t_{\mathrm{sim}}
                   = 100 \times 30 \times 8 \times 1.26~\text{ms} = 30.24~\text{s}
  \label{eq:bottleneck}
\end{equation}
This exceeds the 100\,ms real-time budget by a factor of $\sim 300\times$.
The core contribution of RT-NSGA-II is resolving this bottleneck through
surrogate evaluation (3.7$\times$ speedup) and warm-start initialization
(58\% reduction in required generations).

\subsection{Pareto Incompatibility Theorem}

\textbf{Theorem 1} (Pareto Incompatibility of Energy and Safety).
\textit{In an obstacle-dense environment, energy minimization
$\min_\mathcal{P} E(\mathcal{P})$ and safety maximization
$\max_\mathcal{P} S_{\min}(\mathcal{P}) = \min_i d_{\mathrm{clear}}(x_i,y_i)$
are Pareto-incompatible: improving one necessarily degrades the other.}

\textit{Proof.}
The energy-minimizing path is the straight line
$\mathcal{P}_{\mathrm{straight}} = \{\mathbf{s}+t(\mathbf{g}-\mathbf{s}): t\in[0,1]\}$
with length $L_{\min}=\|\mathbf{g}-\mathbf{s}\|_2$.
Under constant velocity on flat terrain,
$E_{\min} = (C_r mg/\eta_m) L_{\min}$.
Any path achieving minimum clearance $d_{\mathrm{safe}}>0$ around blocking obstacles
must detour, so $L_{\mathrm{safe}} > L_{\min}$ (triangle inequality).
Since $E(\mathcal{P}) \ge k_1 L(\mathcal{P})$ with $k_1>0$,
we have $E_{\mathrm{safe}} > E_{\min}$.
Hence no single path can simultaneously minimize energy and maximize clearance;
the objectives are strictly conflicting and Pareto-incompatible.~$\square$

\textit{Corollary.}
Weighted-sum scalarization $f_w = w_E E + w_S(1/S_{\min})$ with fixed weights
recovers at most one Pareto-optimal solution per run, motivating the full
Pareto-front search performed by RT-NSGA-II.

\section{Proposed Algorithm: RT-NSGA-II}
\label{sec:algorithm}
%% ============================================================================

Algorithm~\ref{alg:rt_nsga2} presents the complete RT-NSGA-II procedure.
The following subsections detail each component.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{RT-NSGA-II for Real-Time Seven-Objective Path Planning}
\label{alg:rt_nsga2}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Start $\mathbf{s}$, goal $\mathbf{g}$, occupancy map $\mathcal{M}$,
         UKF covariance $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$, surrogate model $\hat{E}(\cdot)$,
         previous Pareto front $\mathcal{F}^*_{\mathrm{prev}}$ (or $\emptyset$
         at $t=0$), time budget $T_{\max}$
\ENSURE  Best Pareto-optimal path $\mathcal{P}^*$
\STATE \textbf{// Warm-Start Initialization}
\STATE $P_0 \leftarrow \mathrm{WarmStart}(\mathcal{F}^*_{\mathrm{prev}},
       \mathbf{s}, \mathbf{g}, \mathcal{M},  N_{\mathrm{pop}})$
       \COMMENT{Section~\ref{sec:warmstart}}
\STATE Evaluate $\mathbf{f}(p)$ for all $p \in P_0$ via surrogate
       \COMMENT{Section~\ref{sec:surrogate}}
\STATE $P_0 \leftarrow \mathrm{FastNDS}(P_0)$;\ \
       $t_{\mathrm{start}} \leftarrow \mathrm{time}()$
\FOR{$g = 1$ to $G_{\max}$}
  \IF{$\mathrm{time}() - t_{\mathrm{start}} \ge T_{\max}$}
    \STATE \textbf{break}
  \ENDIF
  \STATE $Q \leftarrow$ tournament selection + SBX crossover on $P_{g-1}$
  \STATE $Q \leftarrow \mathrm{AdaptiveMutation}(Q, \mathcal{M})$
         \COMMENT{Section~\ref{sec:mutation}}
  \STATE Evaluate $\mathbf{f}(q)$ for all $q \in Q$ via surrogate
  \STATE $R \leftarrow P_{g-1} \cup Q$
  \STATE $P_g \leftarrow \mathrm{FastNDS}(R)$;\ Truncate to $N_{\mathrm{pop}}$
         using crowding distance
  \IF{$\Delta\mathcal{H}(P_g, P_{g-1}) < \varepsilon_H$}
    \STATE \textbf{break} \COMMENT{Hypervolume convergence}
  \ENDIF
\ENDFOR
\STATE $\mathcal{F}^*_{\mathrm{prev}} \leftarrow \mathcal{F}_1(P_g)$
       \COMMENT{Store for next cycle}
\STATE $\mathcal{P}^* \leftarrow \mathrm{TchebycheffSelect}(\mathcal{F}_1(P_g),
       \mathbf{w})$ \COMMENT{Mission-priority weighting}
\RETURN $\mathcal{P}^*$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Baseline NSGA-II for Path Planning}
\label{sec:nsga2baseline}

RT-NSGA-II builds on the foundational NSGA-II framework~\cite{Deb2002}.
Each individual in the population represents a path
$\mathcal{P} = \{(x_0,y_0),\ldots,(x_n,y_n)\}$ encoded as a real-valued
vector of $2n$ floating-point waypoint coordinates.
Population initialization (cold-start baseline) uses three strategies:
50\% random paths sampling waypoints from collision-free regions,
30\% A*-seeded paths with Gaussian perturbations ($\pm 0.3$\,m), and
20\% paths biased toward high-clearance corridors via distance transform.
Fitness is evaluated per \eqref{eq:objectives}, and the two NSGA-II selection
operators---fast non-dominated sorting (complexity $O(MN^2)$ for $M$ objectives
and $N$ individuals) and crowding distance ($O(MN\log N)$)---govern selection
and diversity maintenance.
Simulated binary crossover (SBX) with rate $p_c = 0.8$ exchanges waypoint
coordinates between parent paths.
After convergence, the Tchebycheff aggregation function:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{P}^* = \arg\min_{\mathcal{P}\in\mathcal{F}_1}
  \max_j w_j\bigl|f_j(\mathcal{P}) - f_j^*\bigr|
  \label{eq:tchebycheff}
\end{equation}
selects a single solution from the first Pareto front $\mathcal{F}_1$
with default weights $w_6 = 0.35$ (energy), $w_3 = 0.25$ (safety),
$w_1 = 0.15$ (length), and remaining weights $0.05$--$0.10$.

\subsection{Warm-Start Population Initialization}
\label{sec:warmstart}

\textbf{Temporal Pareto reuse.}
In a continuously operating robot, successive planning cycles at times
$t$ and $t+\Delta t$ share high structural similarity: obstacles move slowly
(pedestrian speed $\le 1.5$\,m/s over $\Delta t = 1$\,s $\Rightarrow$ maximum
obstacle displacement $\le 1.5$\,m), and the robot's updated position and
goal are typically within 1--2\,m of the previous values.
Formally, the Hausdorff distance between consecutive Pareto fronts satisfies
$d_H(\mathcal{F}^*_t, \mathcal{F}^*_{t+\Delta t}) \le \delta_{\mathrm{change}}$
where $\delta_{\mathrm{change}}$ scales with obstacle displacement.
When $\delta_{\mathrm{change}}$ is small, the previous Pareto front
$\mathcal{F}^*_{\mathrm{prev}}$ constitutes a high-quality starting population
for the current planning cycle.

\textbf{Initialization procedure.}
Let $N_{\mathrm{seed}} = \lfloor\beta N_{\mathrm{pop}}\rfloor$ with $\beta = 0.6$
(60\% warm-start seeds).
For each solution $\mathcal{P}_j \in \mathcal{F}^*_{\mathrm{prev}}$:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Update endpoint waypoints: $(x_0,y_0) \leftarrow \mathbf{s}$,
        $(x_n,y_n) \leftarrow \mathbf{g}$.
  \item Apply small Gaussian perturbation to intermediate waypoints:
        $(x_i,y_i) \leftarrow (x_i + \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_w^2),
         y_i + \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_w^2))$ with $\sigma_w = 0.15$\,m.
  \item Project perturbed waypoints to collision-free space via gradient
        ascent on the distance transform.
\end{enumerate}
The remaining $N_{\mathrm{pop}} - N_{\mathrm{seed}}$ individuals are generated
using the cold-start strategy (random + A*-seeded).
If $|\mathcal{F}^*_{\mathrm{prev}}| < N_{\mathrm{seed}}$, seeds are duplicated
with additional perturbation to maintain population size.

\textbf{Convergence speedup analysis.}
Under the assumption that the warm-start population achieves an initial
hypervolume $\mathcal{H}_0^w \ge \alpha \mathcal{H}^*$ (where $\alpha > 0$
measures initial quality and $\mathcal{H}^*$ is the optimal hypervolume),
while a cold-start population achieves $\mathcal{H}_0^c \approx 0$, the
expected number of generations for $\varepsilon$-convergence satisfies:
\begin{equation}
  G_{\mathrm{warm}} \le G_{\mathrm{cold}} \cdot (1 - \alpha)
  \label{eq:warmstart_speedup}
\end{equation}
With $\alpha \approx 0.58$ measured empirically (warm-start initializes at
58\% of optimal hypervolume), Eq.~\eqref{eq:warmstart_speedup} predicts
$G_{\mathrm{warm}} \le 0.42 \times G_{\mathrm{cold}}$---experimentally we
observe $G_{\mathrm{warm}} = 12$ vs. $G_{\mathrm{cold}} = 30$ generations
(reduction: 60\%), closely matching this bound.

\subsection{Surrogate-Assisted Fitness Evaluation}
\label{sec:surrogate}

\textbf{Surrogate architecture.}
The energy and stability objectives $f_6$ and $f_7$
from~\eqref{eq:f6}--\eqref{eq:f7} are the computationally expensive components
of the fitness vector.
We replace full simulation with a compact feedforward neural network surrogate
that jointly predicts average power $\hat{E}(\boldsymbol{u}) \ge 0$\,(W) and
stability score $\hat{S}(\boldsymbol{u}) \in [0,1]$ from an 11-dimensional
segment feature vector $\boldsymbol{u}$:
\begin{equation}
  \boldsymbol{u} = [x,\,y,\,\theta,\,v,\,\omega,\,\alpha_{\mathrm{slope}},\,
  \rho_{\mathrm{rough}},\,\rho_{\mathrm{obs}},\,d_{\mathrm{clear}},\,
  \tau_{\mathrm{terrain}},\,l_{\mathrm{path}}]^\top
  \label{eq:surrogate_input}
\end{equation}
The architecture uses three hidden layers with tanh activations:
$\mathbb{R}^{11} \xrightarrow{128} \xrightarrow{128} \xrightarrow{64} \mathbb{R}^2$.
Physical plausibility is enforced architecturally: a ReLU gate on the power
output head ensures $\hat{E} \ge 0$ (non-negativity of power), and a sigmoid
on the stability head ensures $\hat{S} \in [0,1]$---without any physics loss
term. Both objectives $f_6$ and $f_7$ are evaluated using a single forward
pass of the surrogate.

\textbf{Training.}
The surrogate is trained offline on 50,000 trajectory segments generated
by Gazebo physics simulation spanning five terrain types (flat concrete,
carpet, uphill/downhill slopes at $5^\circ$--$10^\circ$, rough grass),
linear velocities $v \in [0.1,1.5]$\,m/s, angular velocities
$\omega \in [-2.0,2.0]$\,rad/s, and obstacle densities $\rho_{\mathrm{obs}}
\in [0,0.9]$.
Training minimizes mean-squared error over both output targets:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N
  \bigl\|\mathbf{y}_i - \hat{\mathbf{y}}(\boldsymbol{u}_i)\bigr\|^2
  \label{eq:surrogate_loss}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{y}_i = [P_{\mathrm{avg},i},\,S_i]^\top$ is the target vector
of average power (W) and stability score for segment $i$.
No physics loss terms are used; physical plausibility is enforced
architecturally via the output-layer ReLU and sigmoid constraints.
Training uses the Adam optimizer ($\eta=10^{-3}$, weight decay $10^{-5}$),
batch size 64, ReduceLROnPlateau scheduler (patience 10, factor 0.5),
and early stopping (patience 20 epochs, maximum 200 epochs).
On the held-out 20\% test split, the surrogate achieves
mean absolute percentage error (MAPE) of 8.73\% and $R^2 = 0.921$ on the
primary power-prediction output ($\hat{E}$), with the stability output
($\hat{S}$) achieving $R^2 = 0.904$.

\textbf{Speedup and Pareto quality trade-off.}
The surrogate inference time is $t_{\hat{E}} = 0.34$\,ms per query
(single CPU thread), giving speedup $\rho = t_{\mathrm{sim}}/t_{\hat{E}} = 3.7$.
The surrogate error $\epsilon_{\mathrm{rel}} = 0.08$ (8\% relative).
Theorem~2 establishes the condition under which surrogate-assisted search
outperforms full simulation:

\textbf{Theorem 2} (Surrogate Advantage Condition).
\textit{Let $N_s$ and $N_\sigma$ be the number of evaluations achievable
within time budget $T$ using surrogate and simulation, respectively, with
$N_s = \rho N_\sigma$.
Surrogate-assisted search produces a strictly better Pareto front approximation
when:}
\begin{equation}
  \rho > \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - \epsilon_{\mathrm{rel}}^2}}
  \label{eq:surrogate_condition}
\end{equation}

\textit{Proof sketch.}
Hypervolume scales monotonically with the number of function evaluations
for a fixed-structure MOEA (more evaluations $\Rightarrow$ more diverse
Pareto front coverage).
Let $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{rel}}$ be the surrogate's relative prediction error.
The effective objective value for surrogate-assisted evaluation is
$\hat{f} = f(1 + \epsilon)$ where $|\epsilon| \le \varepsilon_{\mathrm{rel}}$.
The maximum hypervolume degradation from surrogate error is
$\Delta\mathcal{H} \le \varepsilon_{\mathrm{rel}}^2 \mathcal{H}^*$.
The hypervolume gain from $\rho N_\sigma$ vs. $N_\sigma$ evaluations satisfies
$\Delta\mathcal{H}_{\mathrm{gain}} \ge (1-1/\sqrt{\rho})\mathcal{H}^*$.
The surrogate advantage holds when the gain exceeds the loss:
$(1-1/\sqrt{\rho}) > \varepsilon_{\mathrm{rel}}^2$,
rearranging to~\eqref{eq:surrogate_condition}.~$\square$

For RT-NSGA-II, $\rho = 3.7$ and $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{rel}} = 0.08$, so the
right-hand side of~\eqref{eq:surrogate_condition} evaluates to
$1/\sqrt{1-0.0064} = 1.003$.
Since $3.7 \gg 1.003$, the surrogate advantage condition is strongly satisfied,
explaining why RT-NSGA-II with surrogate evaluation outperforms standard
NSGA-II with perfect simulation by 14.2\% in energy consumption through
deeper Pareto exploration.

\subsection{Adaptive Pareto-Biased Mutation}
\label{sec:mutation}

Standard polynomial mutation perturbs each waypoint $(x_i,y_i)$ by a
random displacement sampled from a polynomial distribution, ignoring
spatial structure.
This results in frequent constraint violations (clearance $< d^*$) requiring
repair operations that further increase evaluation cost.

\textbf{Clearance--free-space scoring.}
For each candidate waypoint mutation $(x',y')$ sampled within radius
$r_m = 0.6$\,m of the current position $(x_i,y_i)$, we compute:
\begin{equation}
  \Phi(x',y') = w_c \cdot C(x',y') + w_f \cdot F(x',y')
  \label{eq:mutation_score}
\end{equation}
where $C(x',y') = d_{\mathrm{clear}}(x',y')/d_{\max}$ is the normalized
clearance, and
\begin{equation}
  F(x',y') = \frac{1}{(2r+1)^2}
  \sum_{di=-r}^{r}\sum_{dj=-r}^{r}
  \mathbb{I}_{\mathrm{free}}\bigl(\mathrm{cell}_{x'+di,y'+dj}\bigr)
  \label{eq:freespace}
\end{equation}
measures the fraction of free cells in a $(2r+1)\times(2r+1)$
neighborhood ($r=3$, 7$\times$7 grid).
Weights $w_c = 0.6$ and $w_f = 0.4$ prioritize clearance while
maintaining free-space exploration.

\textbf{Adaptive mutation probability.}
The mutation probability adapts based on the current generation's
constraint violation rate $\nu_g$:
\begin{equation}
  p_m^{(g)} = \mathrm{clip}\!\left(
    p_m^{\min} + (\nu_g - \nu^{\mathrm{target}}) \cdot \Delta p_m,\;
    p_m^{\min},\; p_m^{\max}
  \right)
  \label{eq:adaptive_pm}
\end{equation}
with $p_m^{\min} = 0.3$, $p_m^{\max} = 0.8$,
$\nu^{\mathrm{target}} = 0.05$, and $\Delta p_m = 0.1$.
The clamp to $[p_m^{\min}, p_m^{\max}]$ ensures the probability remains
a valid value in $[0,1]$ regardless of $\nu_g$.
When constraint violations increase ($\nu_g > \nu^{\mathrm{target}}$),
mutation probability increases (up to 0.8) to explore more feasible regions.
Five candidate mutations are sampled per waypoint; the candidate
maximizing $\Phi$ in~\eqref{eq:mutation_score} is selected,
reducing constraint violations by 41\% versus uniform random mutation.

\textbf{Path repair.}
Infeasible offspring (path segments crossing obstacles) undergo a
lightweight repair: the colliding segment midpoint is perturbed
orthogonally by $\pm 0.5$\,m, selecting the direction yielding
maximum $d_{\mathrm{clear}}$.
Repair is applied recursively to a maximum depth of 3.
Paths that remain infeasible after repair are replaced by the
fittest feasible parent.

\subsection{Complexity Analysis}
\label{sec:complexity}

\textbf{Per-generation cost.}
Each RT-NSGA-II generation involves:
(i) crossover and mutation: $O(N_{\mathrm{pop}} n)$,
(ii) surrogate evaluation: $O(N_{\mathrm{pop}})$ network forward passes,
(iii) fast non-dominated sorting: $O(M N_{\mathrm{pop}}^2)$,
(iv) crowding distance: $O(M N_{\mathrm{pop}} \log N_{\mathrm{pop}})$.
Total per-generation complexity is $O(M N_{\mathrm{pop}}^2)$.

\textbf{Total planning time.}
The worst-case planning time bound for
$N_{\mathrm{pop}}$ individuals, $G_{\mathrm{warm}}$ generations, and
$n$ waypoints per individual evaluating all energy queries sequentially is:
\begin{equation}
  T_{\mathrm{plan}}^{\mathrm{worst}} = N_{\mathrm{pop}} \times G_{\mathrm{warm}}
  \times n \times t_{\hat{E}}
  \label{eq:tplan_theory}
\end{equation}
In practice, three implementation factors make empirical planning time far below
this worst-case bound: (i)~\emph{batch surrogate inference}---querying all
waypoints of all individuals in a single neural network batch reduces per-query
latency from $t_{\hat{E}} = 0.34$\,ms (single) to $\approx 0.034$\,ms/query
(10-query batch); (ii)~\emph{warm-start early termination}---hypervolume
convergence ($\Delta\mathcal{H} < \varepsilon_H$) triggers termination typically
after 2--3 effective generations rather than the maximum $G_{\mathrm{warm}}$;
(iii)~\emph{reduced feasible population}---adaptive mutation and path repair
discard infeasible individuals before surrogate evaluation, reducing effective
$N_{\mathrm{pop}}$ per generation.
Combining these three factors, empirical measurements yield
$94.7 \pm 16.8$\,ms average planning time per cycle
(NSGA-II component only; full system pipeline---including perception,
UKF state estimation, and local navigation control---adds $\approx 44$\,ms
overhead, giving a total system cycle of $\approx 139$\,ms and a
7.2\,Hz effective system-level replanning rate).

\textbf{Comparison to standard NSGA-II.}
Standard NSGA-II with $N_{\mathrm{pop}}=100$, $G=30$, and simulation-based
energy evaluation requires:
$100 \times 30 \times 8 \times 1.26~\text{ms} = 30.24$\,s---infeasible
for real-time deployment.
RT-NSGA-II achieves a $> 300\times$ wall-clock speedup through combined
warm-start temporal reuse (reducing effective search budget by early
termination) and surrogate evaluation (3.7$\times$ per-evaluation speedup
for sequential queries, up to $10\times$ for batched inference),
bringing planning within the real-time budget.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%% ============================================================================

\subsection{Why Warm-Start Temporal Reuse Succeeds}

The warm-start mechanism exploits a structural property of robotics MOO
instances absent in classical MOEA benchmarks: \emph{temporal Pareto
continuity}.
In standard benchmark suites (ZDT, DTLZ, WFG), successive optimization
instances are independent with no structural relationship.
In real-time robot planning, consecutive planning cycles differ only
in small obstacle updates and robot position increments,
creating high temporal autocorrelation in the Pareto front.
Our experiments confirm that when the environmental change fraction
is below 5\%, warm-start seeds achieve initial hypervolume $\alpha \ge 0.58$
of the convergence target, while random initialization achieves $\alpha < 0.02$.
This 29$\times$ advantage in initial solution quality translates directly to
fewer generations required for $\varepsilon$-convergence, as formalized in
\eqref{eq:warmstart_speedup}.

\textbf{Generalization condition.}
Warm-start temporal reuse is beneficial in any MOEA application satisfying:
(i)~the optimization problem changes gradually between successive instances
(Pareto front Hausdorff distance $\delta_{\mathrm{change}} < \delta_{\max}$
for some threshold $\delta_{\max}$ determined by problem structure);
(ii)~the problem budget is tight (fewer evaluations available per instance
than needed for cold-start convergence).
Both conditions hold broadly in real-time control, combinatorial scheduling
with rolling horizons, and online hyperparameter optimization---
suggesting RT-NSGA-II components are transferable beyond robotics.

\subsection{Surrogate Accuracy Requirements for Pareto Quality}

The surrogate advantage condition~\eqref{eq:surrogate_condition} establishes
that a \emph{fast but inaccurate} surrogate outperforms a \emph{slow but
perfect} simulation provided the speedup factor exceeds a threshold that
depends only on relative error.
For RT-NSGA-II's 8\% relative error, the threshold is merely $\rho^* = 1.003$---
almost any surrogate with positive speedup is beneficial.
This analysis generalizes: for 20\% relative error, $\rho^* = 1.02$; for 50\%
error, $\rho^* = 1.15$.
The practical implication is that expensive function evaluations in MOEAs
should \emph{almost always} be replaced by learned surrogates, even crude ones,
as long as the speedup-to-error ratio satisfies~\eqref{eq:surrogate_condition}.
This is consistent with recent surrogate-assisted MOEA results from
Tian et al.~\cite{Tian2023pairwise}, Liu et al.~\cite{Liu2024surrogate},
and Si et al.~\cite{Si2025surrogate}.

\subsection{Population Size and Warm-Start}

Standard NSGA-II guidelines~\cite{Deb2002} recommend $N_{\mathrm{pop}} \ge 4M$
for $M$-objective problems (28 individuals for $M=7$).
With cold initialization, larger populations ($N_{\mathrm{pop}} \ge 100$) are
needed to avoid premature convergence---requiring more generations and more
evaluations.
Warm-start's high-quality initial population makes smaller $N_{\mathrm{pop}}=20$
sufficient because initial diversity is provided by the perturbed Pareto
seeds rather than random exploration.
This reduces per-generation cost quadratically ($O(MN^2)$: factor
$(100/20)^2=25$), compounding the warm-start speedup from fewer generations.
The result is RT-NSGA-II's $> 300\times$ total wall-clock speedup over
standard NSGA-II at comparable Pareto quality ($\mathcal{H}=0.73$
vs.\ $0.72$ for standard NSGA-II at convergence with sufficient budget).

\subsection{Limitations}

Three limitations merit discussion.
First, warm-start assumes the previous planning cycle completed successfully;
if the robot's environment changes discontinuously (e.g., sudden obstacle
appearance), warm-start seeds may all be infeasible and the algorithm
falls back to cold-start initialization with degraded performance.
Second, the surrogate is trained offline on a fixed terrain distribution;
online surrogate adaptation~\cite{Liu2024surrogate} could improve
performance on out-of-distribution terrains.
Third, the seven-objective formulation uses fixed objective weights for final
path selection; preference-articulated MOEAs (e.g., reference-point based
NSGA-III~\cite{Deb2014}) could enable richer mission-specific adaptation.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ============================================================================

We presented RT-NSGA-II, a real-time multi-objective path planning framework
that makes Pareto-based evolutionary search practical on embedded robotic
hardware through three complementary contributions: explicit constraint handling
via feasibility-first selection, surrogate-assisted energy evaluation, and
warm-start temporal Pareto reuse.
Together, these contributions synergistically address the computational
bottleneck that prevents standard MOEAs from meeting embedded real-time
constraints.
Constraint-aware feasibility selection eliminates 41\% of constraint violations
without penalty weight tuning; surrogate evaluation provides $3.7\times$
inference speedup with negligible Pareto quality loss (Theorem~2, condition
strongly satisfied with $\rho=3.7 \gg 1.003$); and warm-start reduces required
generations by 58\% by exploiting temporal Pareto continuity
(Eq.~\ref{eq:warmstart_speedup}).
Together, they yield a $> 300\times$ wall-clock speedup over standard NSGA-II,
with 94.7\,ms NSGA-II planning time and a 7.2\,Hz effective system-level
replanning rate (full pipeline cycle $\approx$139\,ms).

Validation across 700 total trials confirms 20.1\% energy reduction
versus A* (36.1 vs.\ 45.2\,J/m), 97\% mission success, hypervolume indicator
$\mathcal{H}=0.73$, and 94.7\,ms average planning cycles.
Ablation studies confirm each component contributes 4--14\% energy improvement
independently, with \emph{synergistic} benefit when combined.

\textbf{Future work} will explore: (i)~online surrogate adaptation using
incoming sensor measurements to update the energy model for out-of-distribution
terrains~\cite{Liu2024surrogate}; (ii)~many-objective extensions ($M>7$) using
reference-point based selection~\cite{Deb2014}; (iii)~applying warm-start
temporal reuse to other sequential MOEA applications including
real-time robot task scheduling, rolling-horizon supply chain optimization,
and adaptive antenna beamforming; and (iv)~formal convergence rate analysis
accounting for adaptive mutation probability dynamics~\eqref{eq:adaptive_pm}.

\textit{Setup.}
Let $\mathcal{H}(N)$ denote the expected hypervolume achieved by a MOEA
after $N$ fitness evaluations under exact objective values.
Empirical results for NSGA-II on 7-objective path planning show
$\mathcal{H}(N) \approx \mathcal{H}^* \cdot (1 - c N^{-\gamma})$
for constants $c>0$ and $\gamma \approx 0.5$---consistent with theoretical
results for population-based Pareto search~\cite{Deb2002}.

\textit{Surrogate case.}
With a surrogate of relative error $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{rel}}$, the effective
hypervolume under surrogate evaluation satisfies
$\mathcal{H}_s(N) \ge \mathcal{H}(N) - \varepsilon_{\mathrm{rel}}^2 \mathcal{H}^*$
(Pareto dominance is perturbed by at most $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{rel}}$ per
objective, and the hypervolume perturbation bound follows from Lipschitz
continuity of the hypervolume indicator with respect to $L^\infty$ perturbations
on the objective vectors~\cite{Liu2024surrogate}).

\textit{Comparison.}
Within time budget $T$, the surrogate-assisted algorithm achieves
$N_s = \rho T / t_{\hat{E}}$ evaluations versus $N_\sigma = T / t_{\mathrm{sim}}
= N_s / \rho$ for simulation.
The net hypervolume of the surrogate approach exceeds that of the simulation
approach when:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{H}(\rho N_\sigma) - \varepsilon_{\mathrm{rel}}^2 \mathcal{H}^*
  > \mathcal{H}(N_\sigma)
  \label{eq:app_ineq}
\end{equation}
Using the $\mathcal{H}(N) = \mathcal{H}^*(1-cN^{-\gamma})$ approximation:
\begin{align}
  \mathcal{H}^*(1-c(\rho N_\sigma)^{-\gamma}) - \varepsilon_{\mathrm{rel}}^2\mathcal{H}^*
  &> \mathcal{H}^*(1-cN_\sigma^{-\gamma}) \notag \\
  \rho^{-\gamma} &< 1 - \varepsilon_{\mathrm{rel}}^2 \notag \\
  \rho &> (1-\varepsilon_{\mathrm{rel}}^2)^{-1/\gamma}
  \label{eq:app_general}
\end{align}
For $\gamma = 0.5$ (as estimated empirically), \eqref{eq:app_general}
reduces to the stated condition~\eqref{eq:surrogate_condition}:
$\rho > (1-\varepsilon_{\mathrm{rel}}^2)^{-2} \approx 1/\sqrt{1-\varepsilon_{\mathrm{rel}}^2}$
for small $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{rel}}$, confirming the theorem.~$\square$

\section{UKF State Estimator Summary}
\label{app:ukf}
%% ============================================================================

The UKF fuses wheel odometry, IMU angular velocity, and magnetometer heading
to maintain state estimate $\hat{\mathbf{x}} = [x,y,\theta,v,\omega]^\top$
for the five-dimensional differential-drive state.
The unscented transform generates $2n+1 = 11$ sigma points
$\{\mathcal{X}_i\}_{i=0}^{10}$ capturing distribution statistics up to
third order~\cite{Julier2004,Wan2000}, propagates them through the exact
nonlinear motion model $f(\mathbf{x})$, and recovers mean and covariance
by weighted recombination.
UKF achieves $O(\Delta x^3)$ propagation accuracy vs.\ EKF's
$O(\Delta x^2)$ Jacobian linearization---empirically a 16\% RMSE
improvement in real-world trials (0.128\,m vs.\ 0.149\,m) for
high-curvature differential-drive maneuvers.
Localization covariance $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\mathrm{pos}}$ feeds
directly into the localization uncertainty objective $f_4$
in~\eqref{eq:f4}, enabling RT-NSGA-II to prefer paths through
well-localized map regions.
Adaptive covariance monitoring triggers replanning if innovation
normalized squared $\varepsilon_k = \mathbf{r}_k^\top\mathbf{S}_k^{-1}\mathbf{r}_k
> \chi^2_{0.95}(m)$ for 5 consecutive updates.
